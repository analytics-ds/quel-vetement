How do you match colours in an outfit?

By Maxime Coste• June 24, 2026• 7 min read

In front of the wardrobe, the doubt returns every morning: does this green work with this beige? And navy with black? The good news is that colour matching is not an innate talent. It is a mechanism, with a base, a few dosage rules and a handful of combinations that have always worked. Once the method sinks in, you dress faster, not worse.

Neutrals: 80% of the job

White, ecru, beige, grey, navy, brown, khaki, black: neutrals combine with each other in almost any direction. An outfit made of just two or three neutrals is already a successful outfit; that is the secret of men who always seem well dressed with no apparent effort. Beige chinos and a white shirt. Raw denim and a grey jumper. Navy trousers and an ecru polo: none of those combinations requires a second of thought.

Beige deserves a special mention: it is the most flexible neutral in menswear, equally happy with cool and warm tones. A man starting out with colour should build around it, as the example of beige chinos shows, compatible with nearly every top in existence.

The three-colour rule

A legible outfit stops at three colours, accessories included. The most reliable scheme: two neutrals as the base, one accent colour as the touch. Beige chinos, white shirt, pine-green jumper over the shoulders. Raw denim, ecru t-shirt, burgundy socks. The accent draws the eye precisely because it stands alone: two bright colours together fight for attention and tire the outfit.

A practical corollary: patterns count for their dominant colours. A navy-and-white striped shirt occupies two of the three available slots.

Tonal, contrast, temperature: the three pairings that work

The tonal look: several shades of the same family, beige-sand-camel or navy-light blue-grey. Easy to pull off, very elegant, provided the shades are frankly distinct, otherwise the whole thing looks faded.

The light-dark contrast: a light top over a dark bottom, or the reverse. It is the pairing that structures a silhouette, and the reason the white shirt over dark trousers crosses every era.

Temperature last: warm tones (beige, camel, terracotta, burgundy) harmonise naturally with each other, as do cool tones (navy, grey, light blue, seafoam). Mixing warm and cool works too, but demands that one of the two clearly dominates.

The classic traps, and how to avoid them

Black and navy squeezed together first: the pairing only works when the gap is deliberate, a light navy jumper over black jeans for instance. Two tones so close you hesitate look like a mistake. The head-to-toe look in a bright colour next: reserved for runways. Colours near the face last: that is where they are judged. A tone that dulls your complexion up top can live perfectly well below or as an accessory. When in doubt, place the accent far from the face: socks, belt, watch strap.

And to progress fast: photograph your successful outfits. Three months later you will have your own colour chart, more reliable than any theory.

Frequently asked questions

How many colours should you wear at once?

Three at most, accessories included. Two neutrals as the base, plus one accent colour if you feel like it. Beyond that, the outfit becomes hard to read.

Do navy and black go together?

Yes, as long as the difference is deliberate: a navy jumper over black jeans works very well. What never works is a navy and a black so close that it looks like a lighting mistake.

Which colours go with beige?

Almost all of them: white, navy, brown, khaki, burgundy, light blue. Beige is the easiest neutral in menswear and the best base for learning to combine colours.

How do you wear a bright colour without overdoing it?

One bright piece at a time, surrounded by neutrals. A pine-green polo with beige chinos and white sneakers works everywhere. Two bright pieces together is already a gamble.
