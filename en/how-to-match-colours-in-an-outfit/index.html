How do you match colours in an outfit?

By Maxime Coste• June 24, 2026• 7 min read

Matching colours in an outfit comes down to three simple rules: build the look on a base of neutral colours (beige, navy, grey, white, brown), cap the whole outfit at three colours maximum including accessories, and add no more than one bright shade at a time. It is not a gift. It is a method, the same one stylists apply without thinking, and it takes about ten minutes to learn. Everything else, the colour wheel, tonal dressing, light-dark contrast, is refinement. Here are the rules, concrete examples, and a table of combinations that work day in, day out.

The short version, before you open the wardrobe

  • Three colours maximum per outfit, patterns and accessories included.
  • Neutral colours on 60 to 80% of the silhouette, the accent colour as a small touch.
  • A light-dark contrast structures the silhouette: light top over dark bottom, or the reverse.
  • Analogous shades (neighbours on the colour wheel) give the easiest pairings to pull off.
  • When in doubt, keep the strong colour away from the face: socks, belt, scarf.

The colour wheel, your compass for harmonious pairings

The colour wheel arranges shades in the order of the rainbow; painters use it, and so does fashion. The Swiss painter Johannes Itten, who taught at the Bauhaus, formalised its most widely used version in The Art of Color, published in 1961. No need to memorise it. Two readings cover everything menswear requires.

What are analogous colours?

Analogous colours sit next to each other on the wheel: blue, teal, green, or red, orange, yellow. They share a common pigment, which makes their combinations naturally harmonious. Navy and pine green, camel and terracotta, blue-grey and sky blue: pairings that are nearly impossible to get wrong. This is the first family to exploit when you are starting out.

Should you risk complementary colours?

Complementary colours face each other across the wheel: blue and orange, green and red, purple and yellow. The contrast is maximal, therefore striking, therefore risky. My advice: keep one of the two desaturated. Camel chinos (a muted orange, really) with a navy jumper is a complementary pairing half the world wears without knowing it. Bright orange with electric blue is a costume.

Neutral colours: 80% of the job

White, ecru, beige, light grey, charcoal, navy, brown, khaki, black: neutrals combine with each other in almost any direction. An outfit made of two or three neutrals is already a successful outfit, and that is the secret of men who always look well dressed without visible work. Raw denim and a grey jumper. Navy trousers and an ecru polo. None of those combinations requires a second of thought, and they outlive every trend cycle.

Beige deserves its own mention: it is the most flexible neutral in menswear, equally happy with cool and warm tones. A man starting out should build around it, the textbook example being beige chinos, compatible with nearly every top in existence. Brown works on the same logic, one register deeper: dark brown with ecru, light brown with sky blue, chocolate with pale pink. Long declared the enemy of black and navy, it actually pairs well with both.

The three-colour rule (and the 60-30-10 split)

A legible outfit stops at three colours. For proportions, interior designers use the 60-30-10 rule: 60% of the surface for the dominant colour, 30% for the secondary, 10% for the accent. Applied to clothing, that means trousers and coat in the dominant, a top in the secondary, and the accent as a touch. Example: beige chinos and an ecru jacket (60%), navy jumper (30%), burgundy socks (10%). Simple, repeatable, effective.

Aim for balance rather than precision: nobody measures percentages with a tape. What matters is hierarchy. One dominant that owns most of the surface, one secondary shade that breathes, one accent that signs the outfit. Once that reflex is in place, picking the right colours becomes mechanical, and building a coherent look for the office or for a Saturday in town takes two minutes flat. Every other rule in this article is a variation on this one.

A corollary almost everyone forgets: patterns count for their dominant colours. A navy-and-white striped shirt occupies two of the three available slots. A tartan often takes all three by itself, which is why it fights any outfit that already contains colour.

Tonal and monochrome: same-family dressing without the flatness

Tonal dressing means stacking several shades of one family: beige, sand, camel, or navy, sky blue, blue-grey. Easy and very elegant, on one condition: the shades must be clearly distinct, with at least two steps between the lightest and the darkest, otherwise the whole thing looks washed out. Strict monochrome (a single colour head to toe) is more advanced; it works in neutrals, grey or ecru especially, far less in a bright shade. To create relief in a monochrome outfit, play with textures: wool, denim, leather, knit. Three textures can save a single colour.

Pastel tones (pale pink, sage green, sky blue, straw yellow) follow the same logic: one pastel surrounded by neutrals, or a fully assumed pastel tonal look in summer. A pale pink jumper over light grey trousers is one of the most flattering pairings a man can wear. Almost nobody tries it. A shame.

Light-dark contrast, the pairing that structures everything

A light top over a dark bottom, or the reverse: this contrast draws the silhouette and gives the outfit hierarchy, because the eye naturally travels from the light zone to the dark one. It is the reason the white shirt over dark trousers has crossed every era without ageing a day. The same logic applies to your complexion: high-contrast colouring (dark hair, fair skin) carries sharp pairings well, while softer colouring benefits from a smaller gap between light and dark. Thirty seconds in front of a mirror settles the question.

Temperature plays the supporting role: warm tones (beige, camel, terracotta, burgundy, mustard) harmonise naturally with each other, as do cool tones (navy, grey, sky blue, seafoam). Mixing warm and cool works too, provided one of the two clearly dominates. At fifty-fifty, the eye no longer knows where to look.

How do you wear a strong colour without overdoing it?

One bright piece at a time, everything else in neutrals: that is the rule for wearing strong colours (orange, red, emerald green, yellow, fuchsia pink) without turning the outfit into road signage. A burnt orange jumper over ecru chinos and white sneakers is an excellent autumn idea. The same jumper with red trousers is not. Two bright colours fight for attention and wear each other out.

Second lever: surface area. A strong colour in a small dose (beanie, socks, scarf) costs zero courage; over a large surface (coat, trousers) it becomes the centre of the outfit and demands neutrals everywhere around it. Third lever, the most underrated: saturation. Rust orange goes everywhere while fluorescent orange goes nowhere. Most so-called difficult colours turn easy once desaturated: pink becomes dusty rose, green becomes sage, yellow becomes honey.

The classic traps, and how to avoid them

Black and navy pressed together first: the pairing only works when the gap is deliberate, a light navy jumper over black jeans for example. Two tones so close you have to squint read as a lighting accident. The bright-colour total look next: leave it to the runway, and even then. Finally, colours near the face, because that is where they are judged: a tone that kills your complexion up top can live happily on your legs or as an accessory. When in doubt, the accent moves down: coloured socks above white sneakers, a combination nobody has ever ruined.

Another trap, sneakier: following colour trends unfiltered. Every season crowns its shade (a lilac, a pistachio, a cherry red) and shop windows repeat it everywhere. Buy it as an accessory if you like it, never as a coat. An outfit built on neutrals plus one accent stays wearable for five years; an outfit built on the colour of the season comes with an expiry date.

Three tips to improve fast

First tip: photograph the outfits that work. Three months later you will own your personal colour chart, more reliable than any theory, and you will know exactly which base suits you. Second tip: look for inspiration in brand lookbooks rather than runway shows, since stylists have already done the matching work and you only need to observe the proportions. Third tip: when a piece keeps resisting, change what surrounds it before giving it away. A mustard jacket that looks wrong with black turns obvious with ecru and brown.

In the end, matching colours in an outfit boils down to one reflex: neutrals as the base, three colours as the ceiling, one accent at most. Your watch, belt and shoes follow the same score by aligning with the outfit's dominant temperature. The rest is practice, not talent.

Frequently asked questions

How many colours can you wear at once?

Three maximum, accessories included. Two neutrals as the base, plus one accent colour if you feel like it. Beyond that, the outfit becomes hard to read, even when every individual pairing works.

Do navy blue and black go together?

Yes, as long as the difference is deliberate: a light navy jumper over black jeans works very well. What never gets forgiven is a navy and a black so close together they look like a laundry accident.

Which colours go with beige?

Almost all of them: white, navy, brown, khaki, burgundy, sky blue, pine green. Beige is the easiest neutral in menswear, the best base for learning to dress with colour.

What makes a monochrome outfit work?

One colour across the whole silhouette, with at least three different textures for relief: knit, denim, leather for example. Monochrome succeeds far more often in neutrals (grey, ecru, navy) than in a bright shade.

How do you wear a bright colour without overdoing it?

One bright piece at a time, surrounded by neutrals, or a desaturated version of the shade (rust instead of bright orange, sage instead of apple green). Two bright pieces together is a bet you have already lost.
