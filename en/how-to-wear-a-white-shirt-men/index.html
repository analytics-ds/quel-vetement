How should men wear a white shirt?

By Maxime Coste• June 26, 2026• 6 min read

How should men wear a white shirt without looking like a waiter? Three decisions do most of the work: the collar (spread for versatility, button-down for casual), the fabric (smooth poplin around 100 to 120 gsm for dressed-up days, oxford cloth at 130 to 180 gsm for everything else) and what you pair it with (raw denim, chinos, a suit). A well-cut white shirt covers 90 percent of the occasions in an adult life, from a job interview to Sunday coffee. It is the highest-mileage piece in a man's wardrobe, and the one where small details show the most.

In short: what to remember

  • Spread collar for everything, cutaway for formal events, button-down for the weekend.
  • Poplin for dressed-up outfits, textured oxford for casual wear, linen above 25°C (77°F).
  • Shoulder seams on the bone, one fist of ease at the stomach: check the fit with your arms raised.
  • Underneath: an invisible undershirt in heather grey or skin tone, never pure white.
  • Wash at 40°C (104°F) maximum, air dry, treat the collar before every cycle.
  • Budget: expect $40 to $80 for a good mainstream poplin shirt, $100 to $150 from a specialist shirtmaker.

Which collar to choose: the first decision, and the most visible

The collar frames your face. People see it before the brand, before the fabric. The spread collar is the most adaptable option: moderately open points, it works with a tie and holds its shape worn open. The cutaway, wider still, dresses things up and calls for a large tie knot. The button-down collar, borrowed from American polo players around 1900, makes the whole shirt more casual: ideal under a sweater, with jeans, or on its own with the sleeves rolled.

A simple rule for the morning rush: the stiffer and wider the collar, the more formal the shirt. And whatever you paid, a collar that curls up betrays a tired shirt. Replace it, don't defend it.

Fit, size and body type: the difference between wearing and floating

A good white dress shirt follows the torso without hugging it. Three checks in the fitting room: the shoulder seam sits on the bone, a fist fits between fabric and stomach, and the tails stay tucked when you raise your arms. Your body type decides the rest: slim fit if you are lean, regular fit if you are broad, never a slim cut that gapes between the buttons. Gaping at the chest means you need one size up plus a side alteration ($15 to $25 at a tailor, the best money you will spend on clothes).

On transparency, let's settle it once. Under fine poplin, an invisible undershirt in heather grey or skin tone disappears completely, while a white one shows through like a billboard. V-neck or no collar, never a visible crew neck. In summer, when even poplin sticks, a linen shirt takes over with a texture that makes wrinkles part of the look.

Poplin, oxford, twill: which fabric for which occasion?

Poplin, a plain weave with fine crosswise ribs, gives that smooth, slightly silky surface that reads formal. Oxford cloth, heavier and textured, emerged in the 18th century and became the backbone of Ivy style; it makes an everyday shirt that ages beautifully. Twill, soft and supple, barely creases: a smart pick for twelve-hour days and suitcases. My firm opinion: your first white shirt should be poplin, your second oxford. In that order.

A detail that always amuses people: on a men's shirt the buttons are sewn on the right side, a holdover from the 19th century. Check yours.

Five outfit ideas, from most formal to most casual

Under a suit with a tie: the ceremony version, smooth poplin, spread or cutaway collar, crisp ironing. The default for a wedding or an interview, and not up for debate.

Smart casual: shirt tucked into grey flannel trousers or beige chinos, no tie, two buttons open at most. Three is a different kind of evening.

With raw denim jeans: the contrast pairing that has worked since the 1950s, sleeves rolled in two clean folds to mid-forearm. The great Friday classic.

Under knitwear: a button-down collar peeking out of a navy sweater or cardigan, the white brightening the face. Works in photos, works in meetings.

Untucked over chinos in summer: only if the hem is straight and stops at mid-fly. Polished holiday, not nightshirt.

Belt, watch, shoes: the accessories that set the register

White is neutral, so the accessories tell the story. A black leather belt and oxford shoes pull the outfit formal; a woven brown belt and white sneakers bring it back to casual. The useful rule: belt and shoes from the same leather family. The watch follows the same logic, leather strap for the office, steel bracelet or NATO strap on weekends. One subtle touch is enough. A white shirt needs nothing added, which is exactly why it is so easy to live with.

To avoid, in no particular order: a loud novelty tie screaming against the white, a chest pocket bulging with a phone, and the eternal open collar over a gold chain. Register, not costume.

Keeping it white: the care routine that changes everything

A grey or yellowed shirt cancels every styling effort. The protocol takes four steps: treat the collar and cuffs with stain-removal soap before each wash, machine at 40°C maximum, a gentle oxygen-based whitener once a month, air drying on a hanger. Tumble drying yellows the fibres and sets stains. For ironing: medium heat (around 150°C), fabric still slightly damp, starting with the collar and cuffs. Four minutes per shirt, timed. Life expectancy of a shirt worn once a week and cared for properly: three to five years, or 150 to 250 wears.

One last piece of buying advice: two average shirts are worth less than one excellent one. White has no colour or pattern to hide behind, so fabric quality shows immediately. A few fitting-room sessions teach you more than ten guides. Including this one.

Frequently asked questions

Should a white shirt be tucked in or worn untucked?

Tucked in whenever the outfit is dressed up or the shirt has long rounded tails. Untucked only if the hem is straight and stops at mid-fly, over jeans or casual chinos.

Which collar should you choose for a white shirt?

The spread collar is the most versatile: it works with or without a tie and suits most occasions. A button-down collar brings a more relaxed feel, ideal under a sweater or with jeans.

What should you wear under a white shirt?

A close-fitting invisible undershirt in heather grey or skin tone, never pure white and never with a visible crew neck. It absorbs sweat and removes any transparency.

How do you keep a white shirt truly white?

Wash at 40°C maximum with a gentle oxygen-based whitener once a month, air dry, and treat the collar with stain-removal soap before every cycle. Never tumble dry.
