How should you dress for a wedding as a guest?

By Maxime Coste• July 2, 2026• 8 min read

The invitation arrived three months ago, and now that the date is closing in, the question returns: what do you actually wear? Being a wedding guest is a balancing act. Overdressed, and you are treading on the groom's toes. Underdressed, and you look like you mistook the ceremony for a leaving drinks. This guide walks through the guest outfit piece by piece, from the suit down to the socks, with variations for venue, season and dress code.

Start by decoding the dress code

Before opening the wardrobe, reread the invitation. "Cocktail attire" calls for a dark or softly coloured suit with a shirt, tie optional. "Formal" or a religious ceremony in town: dark suit, tie, black or dark brown shoes. "Rustic" or "boho": light suit, soft fabrics, open collar allowed.

And when nothing is specified? Read three clues: the venue (château, town hall, beach, converted barn), the time (a 3pm ceremony wears lighter colours than a 7pm cocktail) and the season. An outdoor summer wedding always pulls the outfit towards light and airy. When in doubt, mid-blue with a white shirt betrays no one.

The guest suit: colour first

Reaching for the black suit is the classic trap. In daytime, black reads sombre and hardens the face in photos. The guest colours that work:

Navy, the safe bet all year round, perfect if you only own one. Light grey, very elegant in spring, easy to warm up with a coloured tie. Beige and sand tones, superb in summer on even slightly tanned skin. Sage green or terracotta, sharper choices that make remarkable guest outfits at a countryside ceremony. To get the garment itself right, cut, shoulders, cloth, our guide on how to choose a suit covers every checkpoint.

As for fabric, the season decides: cool wool in spring and autumn, cotton or linen once the thermometer passes 25 degrees. A linen suit gently creased by the end of the evening has never shocked anyone in a garden in July.

No suit required? The blazer and trousers duo

For a relaxed wedding, a small town-hall ceremony or a next-day brunch, separates do the job beautifully: navy or ecru blazer, beige chinos or flannel trousers, ironed shirt. The look feels less solemn than a suit while staying well above the everyday.

The saving rule: every piece must be impeccable on its own. A blazer that puckers or chinos with bagged knees stand out ten times more on a wedding day than the rest of the year.

Shirt and shoes: the two non-negotiables

The white shirt remains the natural partner of the guest suit: it brightens the face and leaves the colour to the suit and accessories. Light blue as an alternative, fine stripes at a push. Skip the black shirt, whatever the suit.

On the feet, leather: derbies, oxfords or polished loafers, brown with light suits, black or dark brown with dark ones. Shined the night before, soles checked. Sneakers, however spotless, remain a risk: keep them for the dance floor if the couple themselves have set a relaxed tone.

Accessories: where the guest scores points

A matte silk or grenadine tie, one shade darker or brighter than the suit. A pocket square that talks to the tie without copying it. A leather belt matched to the shoes. A slim watch on a leather strap rather than a sports chronograph. Perhaps a floral boutonnière if the theme allows.

One remarkable accessory at a time is plenty. The flamboyant pocket square OR the coloured socks OR the patterned tie: piling them up turns the elegant guest into a circus act.

The mistakes that show in every photo

White and ivory first: those colours belong to the couple, full stop. Even white trousers with a navy blazer flirt with the line. Then come the too-small suit borrowed from your self of ten years ago, the shirt gaping between buttons, sports socks inside derbies, and the tie loosened the moment you leave the church while the group photos are still to come.

Last point, perspiration: a summer wedding lasts twelve hours. Invisible undershirt, a second shirt in the car for the far-sighted, and a jacket in natural fibre that breathes. The guest at ease at midnight is the one who planned at noon.

Frequently asked questions

Can you wear a black suit to a wedding?

Only if the dress code is very formal or the wedding is in the evening. In daytime, black reads like a funeral: navy, light grey or beige instantly feel more appropriate.

Is a suit mandatory for a guest?

No, unless the dress code says so. A blazer with smart chinos, an ironed shirt and leather shoes are plenty for a relaxed or countryside wedding.

Which colours should you avoid at a wedding?

White and ivory, reserved for the couple, all-black in daytime, and any loud print that catches the photographer's lens for the wrong reasons.

Do you have to wear a tie to a wedding?

For a religious ceremony or a formal dress code, yes. For a countryside or summer wedding, a neat open collar works very well, perhaps lifted by a pocket square to keep a dressed-up touch.
